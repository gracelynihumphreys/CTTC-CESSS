May Spotlight: Alan Turing



Source: Wikipedia, (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Turing)

Alan Turing was an English mathematician, logistician, and cryptanalyst widely regarded as the "father of computer science." He is well known for his work in computation, machine, design, code breaking, and artificial intelligence. He is credited with breaking the infamous German enigma code at Bletchley Park during WWII. He also developed the Turing Test, which outlines criteria for determining if an artificial computer is 'thinking.'

Alan Mathison Turing was born on June 23, 1912 in London to Julius Mathison Turing and Ethel Sara Stoney. In 1931, he began attending the University of Cambridge to study mathematics. As a result of his research in probability theory, he was elected to a fellowship at King's College in 1934. During his work on the Entscheidungsproblem, he invented the Turing machine. The Turing machine defined the limits of what can be computed and laid a foundation for modern computers. This device proved that computer algorithms can be solved with a machine. He earned his Ph.D. in mathematics from Princeton (dissertation: "Systems of logic based on ordinals").

When he returned to Britain in the summer of 1938, he joined the Government Code and Cypher school. Soon after, WWII broke out in September of 1939 and organizations were moved to Bletchley Park, Buckinghamshire. At Bletchley Park, many great minds were hard at work decrypting German radio communications. Enigma was the infamous and principal machine used by the Germans to encode their messages. Originally, Bomba was the code-breaking machine used by the Polish and British to decrypt German communications, but the procedures on Enigma were changed in May 1940, rendering Bomba useless.

In response, Turing and others at Bletchley Park designed Bombe, an updated and improved code-breaking machine to combat the Germans. In 1942, Turing created the first systematic method for decrypting German messages. After the war, Turing was made an Officer of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire for his code-breaking service.


Source: Wikipedia, (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombe)

In 1945, Turing began work on the creation of an electronic computer at the National Physical Laboratory in Lonon. His design for the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE) was the first in-depth, complete design of an electronic stored-program digital computer. Though his design would have been faster and contained more storage space than any proposed model at the time, his colleagues thought the engineering was too difficult to physically produce.

Later, in 1948, Turing took the role of deputy director of the Computing Machine Laboratory. During his time there, he designed an input-output system using Bletchley Park tech, designed a computer programming system, wrote the first ever programming manual, and programmed the system used in the Ferranti Mark I (the first marketable electronic digital computer).

Turing was also an early revolutionary in connections between the human brain and computing. He theorized that the human brain was in large part a digital computer and it could be trained to complete computations and logical thinking as it grew and learned.

He also set the standard for what is considered truly artificial intelligence — far before the rise of modern generative AI — that is still used today. He proposed the Turing Test: criterion for determining if an artificial computer was 'thinking' or not.

Turing was elected a fellow of the Royal Society of London in March 1951. However, this was a short lived victory due to the intolerant nature of his country at the time. Turing was gay — a fact he didn't hide from those around him. Most people in his life accepted him and it didn't affect his extraordinary work. However, at the time Britain had laws against what they called "gross indecency" — laws that criminalized homosexuality and violently attacked individuals who weren't heterosexual. To avoid prison, Turing had to endure a series of hormonal "treatments" and because he had a criminal record, he wasn't allowed to hold a government position.

In 1954, Turing died of likely suicide. An apology wasn't issued until 2009. In 2013, Turing was finally granted a posthumous pardon and formal apology.

Turing's legacy is one of genius and innovation. He dared to create concepts, machines, and solutions that had never been dreamed of before. The Turing Test is still used to this day as a determination of if a program is artificially intelligent. Not only was he a genius technically, he was an inspiration in his life, refusing to hide who he was. Turing's legacy reminds modern minds to push the boundaries of the world they live in — to strive for a reality they create themselves, despite the challenges.


April Spotlight: Genevieve Grotjan


Source: NSA, (https://www.nsa.gov/History/Cryptologic-History/Historical-Figures/Historical-Figures-View/Article/1621585/genevieve-grotjan-feinstein/)

Genevieve Grotjan was a brilliant cryptanalyst who made a revolutionary discovery in September of 1940. Grotjan had applied for a civil service job and was hired by William Friedman to work in the Army's Signal Intelligence Service (SIS). She was known as a quiet, methodical person and her major had been in mathematics. She worked on cracking "Purple", a Japanese code system that had perplexed PhDs, Machines, and the western world. "Purple" was the successor to the cipher "Red," and was much more complicated, as it was unlike anything the codebreakers had seen before.

In September of 1940, as Grotjan was analyzing the data from "Purple," she had a breakthrough that allowed the code to be cracked. She reportedly ran to Frank Rowlett, her supervisor, saying, "I've found it." This breakthrough informed U.S. engineers on "Purple" enough to completely reconstruct the machine. Rowlett recognizes her breakthrough as the reason the project was successful. She had cracked the code no one else could - and she had done it by hand. The breakthrough helped to gain instrumental knowledge in both the Atlantic and Pacific during WWII.

Not only was Grotjan's work invaluable in WWII efforts, she also majorly contributed to Cold War efforts. In October of 1943, she was assigned to the Soviet Program and later worked on the project known as "Venona." She designed a process for recognizing the re-use of keys. This process led to the decryption of Russian KGB messages and was said to be "the most important single cryptanalytic break in the history of Venona."

Additionally, she married Hyman Feinstein, a chemist at the National Bureau of Standards, in 1943. She worked as a research analyst for over seven years before eventually resigning from her government position on May 4, 1947. She later became a Mathematics Professor at George Mason University. In 2006, she died at the age of 93 after an extraordinary life of accomplishments. Her husband, Hyman Feinstein established a cryptology award in her honor within the Department of Mathematical Sciences at George Mason.

For years, people didn't know about her or her work for a few reasons. Her work was classified for decades after the war, so the public didn't know about her contributions to cracking "Purple." Additionally, women's contributions to tech and science have long been erased or understated. Due to the classified nature of her work, she went on with her life after the war quietly, marrying and settling down; she never received national recognition for her work or wrote any sort of memoir, just continued on with her life.

Ultimately, Genevieve Grotjan's work was instrumental to the U.S. government in times of conflict and her genius still stands as inspiration today as to just what is possible. Not only is she inspiring many individuals, she stands out specifically as a brilliant and incredible woman for her time. She now inspires current women in STEM with her contributions.
