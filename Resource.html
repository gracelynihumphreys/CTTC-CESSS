Resources



The four professional skills assessed for CRTC are collaboration, communication, innovation, and self direction.

Collaboration

Collaboration is the act of expanding on both team and individual work to create the best possible outcome. Collaboration is extremely important in CRTC, just as it is in any industry or research setting. Students are expected to actively engage in team processes, offer ideas and consider others's viewpoints, work positively with others, and be prepared and responsible when working on a team.


Communication

Communication is the act of sharing and processing ideas through a variety of methods including reading, writing, listening, speaking, observing, presenting, drawing, body language, and social cues. Students are expected to speak clearly and for their audience, convey their ideas and needs through various methods, proactively communicate with the instructor and their peers, and use appropriate language and vocabulary as they would in the workplace. Communication and collaboration often work hand-in-hand.


Innovation

Innovation is the act of discovering new possibilities and solving problems through a mix of being imaginative, diagnosing in a logical manner, researching alternatives, and using creative and critical thinking. Innovation is so important in an engineering and computer science course because students are asked to make creative solutions to problems constantly. They are expected to evaluate and utilize diverse and credible resources for problem solving, apply theory and practice logically, and develop industry solutions with their critical thinking. A piece of innovation that works in tandem with communication is the documentation process in which individuals record their findings, process, and solution while problem-solving or creating.


Self-Direction

Self-direction is the act of reflecting on, regulating, and adapting behavior to the demands of a situation to achieve goals and values. Individuals are supposed to model industry behavior in the classroom, offer and accept constructive feedback and implement it, engage in reflective discussions and make goals, use effective time management, and work efficiently and proficiently. One reoccuring assessment of a student's self-direction skills is Computer Engineering's weekly uniform day, in which each person wears the uniform and earns a grade upon their professional appearance.


The CRTC Uniform:

Every student is required to wear a professional uniform one day a week for CRTC's Computer Science program. The uniform is made up of a customized shirt that is purchased at the beginning of the course, black dress pants (or khakis), a belt, and any pair of closed-toe work shoes.

Wearing the uniform teaches students responsibility over their appearance and self-direction, how to dress professionally, and what dressing appropriately and confidently in a job looks like. Each student earns a weekly self-direction grade based upon their appearance during uniform day. The critera is laid out in a rubric on the Computer Engineering website.

Because purchasing the uniform shirt and acquiring the other uniform pieces is is a requirement for the course, we have compiled resources to dress confidently in the most affordable manner possible. The resources laid out below are options and suggesions on how to dress your best affordably and accessibly! If the cost of the uniform shirt is prohibitive to you for any reason, please to reach out to Mrs. Marcou at lmarcou@sau8.org or to the creators of this page! The cost of a uniform should not be prohibitive to full participation in this program and in STEM, so take advantage of these resources and ask about any questions or concerns!

What Does it Mean to Dress Professionally?

The goal of these uniforms is to learn how to dress professionally, so you can put your best foot forward in your career. Though the uniform is standardized for consistency across the classroom, each student is an individual whose presentation of professionalism may vary based on their personality, physical features, and more. All expressions and appearances are valid as long as you are putting your best foot forward into the professional world. This means wearing a uniform you can feel confident in, keeping an appearance that is neat and functional for your role, and dressing consistently with your position and identity. By dressing appropriately and confidently, each student can learn to feel at home in their environment and in their own identity as the professional they are working to become.

It is important to awknowledge that STEM has historically restricted access to minorities, preventing individuals from joining communitites and projects based on their race, ethnic or cultural background, gender, and more. This is why it is important to be mindful of our intentions and choices around what is considered professional. The traditional idea of professionalism in STEM has was historically a masculine and eurocentric look and viewpoint. However, this community is all about acceptance and excellence in STEM, not about upholding outdated traditions that harm the STEM community.

As such, it is important to remind all students that you belong in STEM and a person of any race, ethnicity, gender, or background can be a successful professional without changing themselves to fit into outdated and prejudiced expectations. Natural hair is professional. Traditional clothing is professional. There is no unprofessional color or style so long as it fits within the expectations of the work you are completing for your job or your class. Basically, the most important thing is your absolute right to be you and pursue your dreams in STEM in the way that makes most sense for you as an individual.
